# The Pathway Mixture Model (PMM)

The describe rung of the research program. The goal is a single generative process such that
two traits (1) look independent in aggregate, (2) collide concordantly at the joint tail, and
(3) generate the demarcation test as a prediction rather than an assumption. A contrasting
Berkson-collider generator is given as the foil, and the pair defines a falsification engine.

The model's *construction* follows from the axioms (it is derived). Its *empirical adequacy* —
whether real data are generated this way — is open. Hence status: provisional.

## The model (a fork)

The population is a mixture over a hidden regime $Z$:

$$Z = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{w.p. } 1-\pi \quad \text{(the bulk — "the plains")} \\ 1 & \text{w.p. } \pi \quad \text{(the pathway — "the trail")} \end{cases}$$

**Bulk** $(Z=0)$: the traits are independent, $X \perp Y$, each $\sim \mathcal N(0,1)$.

**Pathway** $(Z=1)$: the traits are co-generated by a shared latent factor $P$ (a common cause),

$$X = \mu_x + a\,P + \varepsilon_x, \qquad Y = \mu_y + b\,P + \varepsilon_y,$$

with loadings $a,b>0$, idiosyncratic noise $\varepsilon_x,\varepsilon_y$ independent, $\mathbb E[P]=0$.

**Co-effect** (an un-selected third trait, used only for the demarcation prediction):

$$W = \mu_w + c\,P + \varepsilon_w.$$

We never condition on $W$. It exists in the model so the model can be asked whether it predicts
an un-selected co-effect.

### Parameter dictionary (every parameter is a theoretical quantity)

- $\pi$ — **pathway prevalence**: how rare the trail is.
- $a, b, c$ — **loadings**: how strongly the pathway bundles each trait.
- $\mu_x, \mu_y, \mu_w$ — **tail offsets**: how far up the trail climbs each axis.
- $\operatorname{Var}(P)$ — pathway dispersion.
- $\nu$ — **tail index** (if $P$ or $\varepsilon$ is Student-$t$): heaviness of the tail. $\nu\to\infty$ recovers the Gaussian limit.

### Why it is the three axioms

- **Axiom 1** (extremes are reached by pathways): rarity $\pi$ + offset $\mu$ encode "few roads, far up."
- **Axiom 2** (pathways bundle): the shared $P$ with loadings $a,b,c$ *is* the bundling; $W$ shows it reaches a trait nobody selected on.
- **Axiom 3** (tail-localized, concordant): dependence exists only in the rare $Z=1$ regime → independence in the bulk; $ab\operatorname{Var}(P)>0$ → concordant.

## Result 1 — it looks independent in aggregate

$$\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y) = \pi\,ab\operatorname{Var}(P) + \pi(1-\pi)\,\mu_x\mu_y.$$

Both terms are $O(\pi)$. (Derivation: $\mathbb E[XY]=\pi(\mu_x\mu_y+ab\operatorname{Var}(P))$ and
$\mathbb E[X]\mathbb E[Y]=\pi^2\mu_x\mu_y$; subtract.) For small $\pi$ the population correlation is
tiny — the kind of number dismissed as noise.

## Result 2 — it collides at the tail

Let $\lambda(q)=\mathbb P\!\left(Y>y_q \mid X>x_q\right)$ be the conditional exceedance at quantile $q$.
Decompose the joint exceedance by regime:

$$\mathbb P(X>x_q,\,Y>y_q) \approx \underbrace{(1-\pi)\,\mathbb P_{\text{bulk}}(X>x_q)\mathbb P_{\text{bulk}}(Y>y_q)}_{\text{factorizes} \to (\text{tail})^2,\ \text{vanishes fast}} \;+\; \underbrace{\pi\,\mathbb P_{\text{path}}(X>x_q,\,Y>y_q)}_{\text{shifted up and correlated}}.$$

The bulk term dies quadratically in the tail probability. The pathway term does not, because the
pathway population is offset into the tail and internally correlated. So at high $q$ the joint tail
is *dominated* by the pathway, and $\lambda(q)$ stays $O(1)$ where independence would send it to $0$.
Those are the "impossible" collisions.

## Result 3 — the tail-localization is the diverging ratio (the crisp form of Axiom 3)

Aggregate dependence is $O(\pi)$ (Result 1). Tail enrichment is $O(1)$ (Result 2). Therefore

$$\frac{\text{tail enrichment}}{\text{aggregate correlation}} \;\longrightarrow\; \infty \quad \text{as } \pi \to 0.$$

This is the exact statement of "independent in the body, dependent in the tail." It also yields a
non-obvious corollary worth stating on its own:

> **The rarer the pathway, the more deceptive it is** — fainter in aggregate, more shocking at the
> tail. Rarity does not weaken the effect; it hides the cause while sharpening the symptom.

That corollary is the formal version of the original intuition: fewer samples at the fringe, yet a
strangely high propensity to collide.

## Result 4 — the Gaussian subtlety (stated honestly)

There are two senses of "tail dependence," and they behave differently:

- **Practical** (finite $q$): $\lambda(q)$ sits far above the independence baseline. Robust, observable,
  and the effect you actually see. Holds *even with Gaussian components*.
- **Asymptotic** ($q\to 1$): $\lambda_U=\lim_{q\to1}\lambda(q)$. If every component is Gaussian, then
  $\lambda_U=0$ — a bivariate normal has **no** asymptotic tail dependence at any correlation $<1$.
  To get $\lambda_U>0$ you must give the pathway heavy tails (let $P$ or $\varepsilon$ be Student-$t$
  with finite $\nu$).

The honest foil/null is the **Gaussian copula**: $\lambda_U=0$ for all $\rho<1$ — the model that
assumed joint extremes away and was destroyed in 2008. For a Student-$t$ copula the closed form is

$$\lambda_U = 2\,t_{\nu+1}\!\left(-\sqrt{\tfrac{(\nu+1)(1-\rho)}{1+\rho}}\right),$$

which is $>0$ for finite $\nu$ and $\to 0$ as $\nu\to\infty$. Claim only the practical effect unless
heavy tails are independently justified.

## Result 5 — the model derives the demarcation test

Condition on the joint tail $\{X>x_q,\,Y>y_q\}$. Because that tail is dominated by $Z=1$, and
$W=\mu_w+cP+\varepsilon_w$ loads on the same $P$,

$$\mathbb E[\,W \mid X,Y \text{ both extreme}\,] \;\gg\; \mathbb E[W].$$

The un-selected co-effect is elevated, concordantly. This is the demarcation signature falling out
of the model, not bolted on.

## The foil: a Berkson-collider generator

Population with **no** $P$: $X\perp Y$, each $\sim\mathcal N(0,1)$, and an un-selected $W\perp(X,Y)$.
A selection node $S$ is then conditioned on. Two cases, with **different** signatures:

- **Additive / compensatory gate**, $S=\mathbf 1\{X+Y>\tau\}$: within $S{=}1$, $\operatorname{corr}(X,Y)<0$
  (the classic Berkson negative correlation — strongest near the boundary). $\mathbb E[W\mid S]=\mathbb E[W]$.
- **Conjunctive gate**, $S=\mathbf 1\{X>a \wedge Y>b\}$ (a product region): within $S{=}1$, $X$ and $Y$ stay
  **independent** (the region factorizes), though both are marginally elevated. $\mathbb E[W\mid S]=\mathbb E[W]$.

In neither case is the un-selected $W$ elevated, because there is no common cause to reach it.

## Diagnostic battery (the falsification engine)

The discriminating move is to **condition on a single trait's extreme and look at the others** —
that is where the fork is unique.

| diagnostic | independence | additive collider | conjunctive collider | pathway (fork) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| aggregate $\operatorname{corr}(X,Y)$ | $0$ | $0$ (uncond.) | $0$ (uncond.) | small $+$, $O(\pi)$ |
| $\mathbb E[Y\mid X>x_q]$ vs $\mathbb E[Y]$ | flat | flat | flat | **rises** (concordant) |
| $\lambda(q)$ at high $q$ | $\to 0$ | low | low | **high** |
| $\operatorname{corr}(X,Y)$ within selected $S$ | — | **negative** | $0$ | positive |
| co-effect $\mathbb E[W\mid \text{tail}]$ | baseline | baseline | baseline | **elevated** |
| casualty roster | n/a | nameable | nameable | none |

The two rows that no collider can fake: a single-trait condition lifting an *un-selected* trait
($\mathbb E[Y\mid X>x_q]$ and $\mathbb E[W\mid\text{tail}]$). Those are the corporeal signature.

## Simulation spec (deliverable)

Draw $N\approx 10^6$. Outputs:

1. **Scatter** $(X,Y)$ colored by hidden $Z$, with the joint-tail box overlaid — the pathway points
   visibly populate the corner the bulk cannot reach.
2. **Topographic joint density** (reuse the existing matplotlib gradient maps): the tall bulk peak at
   the origin plus the faint pathway ridge running into the tail corner.
3. **Conditional-exceedance curve** $\lambda(q)$ vs $q$, three lines: independence, collider, fork.
4. **Co-effect elevation**: distribution of $W$ in the joint tail vs the population.

Illustrative defaults (label as illustrative): $\pi=0.01$, $a=b=1.2$, $\mu_x=\mu_y=2.5$,
$\operatorname{Var}(P)=1$, $\varepsilon\sim\mathcal N(0,0.5^2)$; co-effect $c=1.0,\ \mu_w=1.5$;
heavy-tail variant $P\sim t_4$. At these values aggregate $\operatorname{corr}\approx 0.07$ while
$\lambda(0.99)$ runs roughly an order of magnitude above the $0.01$ independence baseline — small
cause, loud symptom. Shrinking $\pi$ widens that gap (Result 3).

## Variants and open questions

- **Network / mutualism variant**: replace the single $P$ with mutually reinforcing traits (a coupled
  dynamical or graphical model), per the research program's stance that a pathway may be a network,
  not only a factor. Same tail signature is conjectured; unproven here.
- **Multivariate**: $k$ traits with a loading vector; the joint tail becomes a cone, and the co-effect
  test generalizes to "every un-loaded-on trait that still rises."
- **Estimation** (open): recover $\pi, a, b, \mu, \nu$ from data via mixture EM plus tail estimation,
  and test the practical-vs-asymptotic distinction empirically.
