# Primer — The Positive Manifold, *g*, and Mutualism

*Background prior art. A century-old, heavily-studied empirical regularity FTH uses as its first
real test bed — named as established work, not claimed.*

## The empirical regularity

Charles Spearman (1904) observed that scores on diverse cognitive tasks all **correlate
positively** with one another — verbal, spatial, numerical, and so on. No matter which pair you
pick, the sign is positive. This pattern is the **positive manifold**, and it is one of the most
robust findings in psychology.

## Two accounts of the same pattern

The positive manifold is exactly a *concordant* positive correlation produced by some generative
structure — which makes it the cleanest place to "trace a pathway." The literature offers two
competing structures, and a century of debate between them:

- **A latent general factor — Spearman's *g*.** A single hidden cause loads on every task; the
  shared variance *is* `g`. In FTH's language this is a **fork**: one common cause, arrows fanning
  out to many observed traits, producing concordant correlation. This is the textbook
  factor-analytic reading.
- **Mutualism / network models.** van der Maas and colleagues (2006) proposed that abilities start
  out uncorrelated but **mutually benefit one another during development** — reading aids
  vocabulary aids reasoning — so the positive manifold *emerges* from reciprocal interaction
  **without** any single underlying `g`. This is the network-psychometrics account.

## Why this is the test bed FTH starts from

FTH deliberately does **not** take sides. Its term *pathway* denotes *any real generative
structure that bundles traits* — a single latent factor **or** a self-reinforcing network. That
keeps the framework off a hill psychometricians have contested for a hundred years, while
preserving the core claim: the bundling is **mechanistic, not a selection artifact**. The positive
manifold is a fork (or network) sitting openly in the established literature — far cleaner ground
for tracing a real pathway than any business or sports anecdote. See `research-program` for how the
"trace → describe" rungs build on it, and the demarcation-passing predictions it suggests
(tail-tightening; non-cognitive co-effects).
