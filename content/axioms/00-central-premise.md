# Central Premise

When you select an individual for an extreme value on some trait, you have drawn one of two
kinds of traveler, and you cannot yet tell which: a *lucky bulk draw* — a rare, uncorrelated
coordinate that happened to land high — or a traveler who arrived by a *generative pathway* that
carries a syndrome of correlated traits. A single extreme is weak evidence between them, but not
zero: because the pathway population is offset further into the tail, the deeper you go on one
axis the larger the share of travelers who came by a pathway.

The decisive evidence is not the single extreme — it is the *collision*. Conditioning on one
extreme makes other, seemingly unrelated extremes far more common than independence predicts,
and that is the thing a lucky bulk draw cannot fake: independence forbids two uncorrelated
extremes from co-occurring above the product of their rates, so a syndrome of them is the
signature of a shared pathway. The "impossible" co-occurrences at the tails are the visible
signature of invisible shared pathways.

## The picture to hold

Down on the plains — the bulk of the distribution — you can wander in any direction, and two
traits are uncorrelated. There are *many* trails toward the summit, and most who get high up
came by an ordinary one, arriving with no particular kit and nothing in common. But a few of
those trails are different: they carry cargo, and everyone who comes up one of *them* arrives
carrying the same kit. You cannot pick the cargo-carriers out by altitude alone — a lone climber
high on one face might have taken any trail. You spot them where the trails *converge*: the
travelers who arrive high on several faces at once are the ones who came up a cargo trail,
because no ordinary trail reaches more than one. The correlation isn't on the plain, and it
isn't simply "at the top" — it's at the *meeting point*, where extremes coincide.
