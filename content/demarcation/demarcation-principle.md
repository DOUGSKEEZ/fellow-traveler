# The Demarcation Principle

This is the falsifier — the line that separates a real Fellow Traveler collision from a known
fallacy (Berkson's paradox / collider bias). Without it, the whole framework is indistinguishable
from "you conditioned on a collider and were surprised the sample is correlated."

## The principle (compressed)

> A correlation among extremes is a **Fellow Traveler collision** — not a Berkson artifact —
> when its members share a **co-effect**: a further trait no filter selected for, elevated in
> the same direction.
>
> *Colliders share their inputs; pathways share their outputs.*

## Two operational tests

A cluster passes as a real pathway effect when it passes **both**:

1. **The co-effect test (inputs vs. outputs).** Do the members share traits *beyond* the ones
   the selection screened on? A filter's shared traits are a **closed** set — exactly the
   inputs to the gate. A pathway's shared traits are an **open** set — the syndrome, including
   co-effects nobody selected for. The presence of even one un-selected, concordantly-elevated
   co-effect is the signature.

2. **The casualty test.** Can you enumerate who was rejected? A filter has a **nameable**
   casualty roster (failed applicants, eliminated candidates) because there was a submission
   event. A pathway has **no roster** — nobody applied; the syndrome simply emerged or did not.
   Nameable casualties → you are looking at a filter. No roster → you are looking at the
   pathway itself.

The co-effect test tells you a pathway is *present*. The casualty test tells you that you are
looking at the pathway *itself* and not merely at a gate stacked on top of it.

## Honest asymmetry (do not paper over)

The test **confirms strongly but disconfirms weakly.**

- Finding a shared, un-selected co-effect strongly confirms a pathway, because a pure collider
  has no common cause upstream and therefore *cannot* manufacture one.
- Finding none only weakly denies a pathway, because severe range restriction (observing only
  the extreme survivors) attenuates correlations and can hide a real co-effect from view.

A null result is suggestive, not decisive. This asymmetry is itself an open question — see the
open-gaps layer.
