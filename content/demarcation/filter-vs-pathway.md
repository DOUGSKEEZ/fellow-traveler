# Filter vs. Pathway

Two structures can produce the same observed thing — a selected group whose members share
several traits. They are distinguished by *why* the members share those traits and *whether the
sharing is real*. The framework keeps these two words strictly apart.

## Definitions

**Filter** — an *exogenous*, explicit selection gate imposed on a population from outside.
Causal shape: a **collider** (arrows converge *in*). Its members share their **inputs** — a
*closed* set, exactly the traits the gate selected on — and within the selected set those
inputs relate *compensatorily* (a deficit in one is offset by a surplus in another). The
correlation exists only inside the selected set and vanishes in the full population. A filter
has an **enumerable casualty roster**.

**Pathway** — an *endogenous*, implicit generative process that produces individuals. Causal
shape: a **fork** / common cause (arrows fan *out*). Its members share their **outputs** — an
*open* set, the syndrome, including co-effects nobody selected for — and the correlation is
genuine wherever the pathway is active; conditioning on the extreme merely makes it visible. A
pathway has **no enumerable casualty roster**.

A useful shorthand: the filter is **explicit** (a gate in the world); the pathway is
**implicit** (a process in the individual).

## The contrast

```
COLLIDER  (a filter — Berkson)            FORK  (a pathway — Fellow Traveler)

   A    B    C   ← traits                          P     ← one common cause
    \   |   /      (independent in                / | \ \
     \  |  /        the population)              /  |  \  \
       v v v                                    A   B   C   D   ← traits
      [  S  ]   ← the gate                        (same kind of traits)
                  you condition on
   selected set = people who passed S        observed group = people who "have P"
                                             (e.g. the ones sitting at the extreme)

  members all carry A, B, C                 members carry A, B, C ... and D, E too
  ONLY because the gate demanded them       because P generated ALL of them
  the correlation lives INSIDE S            the correlation lives wherever P is active
  (it vanishes in the full population)      (the extreme just makes it visible)
  casualties (failed S): you can name them  casualties: none — nobody applied
```

The lettered nodes are **traits** (shared characteristics), never people. The "selected set"
and the "observed group" are the people. The only structural differences between the two
panels are the direction of the arrows and what sits at the hub. The "... and D, E too" on the
fork side is the entire demarcation test: the filter's shared list is closed, the pathway's is
open.

## The epigram

> Colliders share their inputs; pathways share their outputs.

## Overdetermination (the hard case)

A brutal, multi-trait filter can stack on top of a pathway, and then both signatures
superimpose. Special-forces selection, for example, is *both* a conjunctive physical gate (a
filter) *and* a draw from a pre-existing dispositional population (a pathway). The co-effect
test isolates the pathway's contribution: look for a trait the graduates share that the gate
neither tested nor required.

**Hazard:** brutal filters covertly select on far more than they advertise. The live failure
mode is mislabeling a covertly-selected trait as "un-selected" and crediting a pathway with
something the filter quietly screened for. The rigor is entirely in how confidently you can
state what the selection *actually* conditions on.
